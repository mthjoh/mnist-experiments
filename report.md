## 2. Network Implementation

I created the layers.py file to simulate the different layers in the neural network: Affine, Relu, and SoftmaxWithLoss. The Affine class's forward method returns the dot product of the input and the weights with the biases added in. Its backward method stores the derivative of the weights and biases in relationship with the loss and returns the gradient with respect to its input.  The Relu class's forward method returns the array with all the negative values zeroed out. The backward pass returns the backprop with all the masked values set to 0. The SoftmaxWithLoss's forward method applies the softmax and cross entropy error functions to the received data and returns it. Its backward method returns the difference between the prediction and the true label divided by the batchsize. I created network.py to simulate forward passes and backpropagation. MultiLayerNet builds the layer chain from a sizes list such as [784, 100, 10]. The train.py file handles the training of the network.  

While briefly comparing wide nets and deep nets, I discovered that deep layer nets failed after a certain amount of depth - the loss was stuck at 2.302 (equal to -log(0.1) the loss of uniform guessing among 10 choices). With naive init (weight = small random * 0.01), the signal shrank at every layer(~400:1 by the last layer). This resulted in the signals reaching the softmax with loss function while being too small. The gradients came back as zeros. This taught me the importance of He initialization: scaling each layer's weights by the root of (2/n_inputs) specifically designed for ReLU networks. Immediately the same deep architecture was able to be trained; a 14 layer net reached its full accuracy.

Backprop needed double checking to make sure the network was working correctly. Gradient checking was used to compare its results with the results of backprop. The formula was (loss up by ε − loss down by ε) / 2ε, ε being a small number (in this case 1e-4). ε could not be too small such as 1e-300 because python uses float64 setting a limit to precision. The result of the comparison was that the backprop was accurate up to ~7 decimal points, checked on both W and b across multiple layers. 

The first working network — plain SGD, [784,100,10] — reached 97.99% after 100k iterations. 
   
## 3. Optimization Experiments

I implemented three types of optimizers (SGD, Momentum, and Adam) on the same interface making it easy for us to swap between different optimizers. All three use the method update (params, grads), making swapping optimizers a one line change. Here is a summary of each optimizer: SGD - subtract lr*gradient; Momentum - accumulate velocity so consistent direction builds speed (v = mom*v - lr*grad, param+=v); Adam - adaptive per parameter step with bias correction. For this specific example ([784,100,10], 10k iterations, and a lr of 0.001 for Adam (m̂/√v̂ ≈ 1, so every step is ≈ lr regardless of gradient size)), Momentum was trained slightly more accurately than Adam most of the time (ex. Momentum: 0.9801, Adam: 0.9779, SGD: 0.9733, all with fluctuation due to noise). This may be due to the high quality of data present in MNIST. Adam may be more effective with data with more noise. 

A batch size experiment was also carried out with batch 10, 100, and 1000 at a fixed lr of 0.1 with Momentum. The result was batch 10 collapsing to ~0.64 accuracy whereas batch 100 and 1000 hovered around roughly ~0.98 accuracy. The lesson was that noises scale around roughly 1/root(batch), tiny batches gave highly fluctuating estimates which was amplified by Momentum. 

Using this rule, I scaled down the lr according to the batch size. 0.01 / 0.1 / 0.5 for batches 10 / 100 / 1000. The accuracy of batch 10 rose to ~0.97. However, the results for batchsize 1000 dropped slightly to ~0.9752 compared to what it was - around 0.9781. Later, batch 500 at lr 0.5 produced loss NaN and an accuracy of ~0.098. When lr is too big, the weights land at somewhere worse, resulting in a larger gradient. This creates a vicious cycle until the numbers exceed the floating range. Once a weight is NaN, every later computation becomes NaN, which is why the network stuck at random guessing. Batch 500 at lr 0.1 gave the project's best result. The lesson learned was that lr scales with batch size only up to a certain point - past it and the training can diverge. 

Another experiment on width vs depth was conducted, this time more appropriately. The experiment compared [784,300,10] vs [784,190,190,190,10], both around 238k parameters. Having the same amount of parameters made sure that the shape was being compared, not the size. The experiment was run twice and the result was 0.9793 (run 1), 0.9787 (run 2) (width) vs 0.977 (run 1),  0.9802 (run 2) (depth), a statistical tie. Width and Depth (given that He init is applied) did not necessarily matter at least in this specific case as long as the parameters were roughly similar. 

## 4. Performance Engineering

Accuracy experiments tell us what increases and decreases the accuracy. But optimization requires us to count the costs. perf.py does just that. time.perf_counter measured the start and finished time giving me the time elapsed during the training. Using elapsed, I calculated iters/sec and images/sec. The FLOPs (FLOPs = operations, FLOPS = operations per second) were counted analytically and not measured. Each matmul of (N,a)@(a,b) costs around 2·N·a·b. Using this fact, I added up each matmul to get the forward pass and multiplied it by three for the standard approximation of forward+backward. GFLOPS was simply dividing FLOPs with the elapsed time and dividing that value with 1e9 (G means Giga). Compare GFLOPS to what the specific chip can theoretically do and I can get the utilization. This is standard practice used across AI labs. 

The first measurement was on a network of shape [784,100,10], batch 100, and 1000 iterations. The result was time: ~0.315s, iters/sec: ~3170, images/sec: ~317047 GFLOPS: ~151. An M5 chip reaches roughly 1,790 GFLOPS peak in float32 through its matrix coprocessor (AMX), which is what NumPy's matmuls use. Against that, 151 GFLOPS is ~8.4%. There are a couple of reasons for the low performance. First, the matrices are small. Peak benchmark matrices multiply 1000s by 1000s. So hardware sits idle. The timer also includes work that isn't counted as FLOPS such as ReLU, softmax, optimizer updates, etc.

The third suspected reason was that the weights were float64. The 1790 GFLOPS peak is a float32 number. M5's float64 matrix throughput is roughly 3.8 times lower at 470 GFLOPS. Against that 151 GFLOPS is closer to 32%. Deep learning rarely needs float64 so I decided to cast every weight and bias to float32. The result was rather surprising: it got slower, ~0.35s vs ~0.32s, and the gap was consistent across repeated runs. A probe was used to diagnose the issue and the probe printed the gradient dtype as float64. net.W[j] = net.W[j].astype(float32) created a new array and pointed the list slot at it but each Affine layer was still referencing the original float64 array. Not only was the result slower but the optimizer was updating arrays that the forward pass never read - the network was not training at all. The solution was to re-point each layer to the new array (net.layers[2*j].W = net.W[j]). The result was the following: Time: ~0.193s, iters/sec: ~5156, images/sec: ~515634 GFLOPS: ~245 (~14% utilization), around 1.62 times faster. Float16 was also tried out of curiosity. The result: Time: ~23.629s, iters/sec: ~42, images/sec: ~4231 GFLOPS: ~2. There was no hardware path for float16 which made NumPy fall back on slow generic code. Lesson: only lower precision when the hardware accelerates it. 

For the dtype float32, I increased the batch to 2000 and the sizes [784, 2000, 2000, 10], the GFLOPS went up to ~809 (45% utilization). Same hardware and same dtype but bigger matrices and batchsize keep the hardware occupied. Not only are bigger batches better for stability of the gradient, they also use the hardware more efficiently allowing for more training to be done in the same amount of time. 

## 5. The 99% Campaign

Breaking 99% accuracy seemed like a worthwhile pursuit so I used everything I learned so far: Momentum, float32 speed, scaled batch/lr, more capacity. The previous record was held by a network with the following specs: Momentum, float64, 100 batches, 0.1 lr, mom=0.9, [784,1000,1000,10], iteration 10000, and a resulting accuracy of 0.9861. So for the new model, I used the following specs: Momentum, float32, 500 batches, 0.1 lr, mom=0.9, [784,1000,1000,1000,10], iteration 20000. The result was surprising: Accuracy:0.9834, Loss:1.115598661272088e-05. The underwhelming result was explainable by the Loss of ~1.12e-5. With 3.6M parameters and a loss 1.1e-05, the network had essentially memorized the data set. The network had essentially perfect training performance while the accuracy stalled. The capacity and the iterations were not the constraints in this scenario, the data was. 

The solution to this problem was to shift the image data around. The batch was first reshaped into (N,28,28). The following step involved shifting the image data up, down, left, right, or none (Not moving the image at all was also a variant that the model needed training on). It is important to note that the labels were not shifted, only the images were. The labels need to be the same because a shifted 7 is still a 7. A limitation worth stating is that the images were shifted per batch. A batch of 500 images were all shifted towards the same direction. Individual shifting would have given more variation in the data. The new data pool of the original and shifted images effectively created 5 times more data for the network to train on. The result was an accuracy of .9892, a new record with a rise in training loss to ~7e-5. It wasn't quite 99% yet and the memorization issue wasn't quite solved at this point. I needed to increase the data pool even more. 

The new augmentation method involved two dx and dy, each representing the shift horizontally and vertically, each ranging from -3 to 3. This results in 49 possible shifts including diagonals. One important implementation detail worth stating is that the shift must be done sequentially through a fresh intermediate array. Without the fresh intermediate array, the augmentation smudges each image, contaminating the data. The result of the new implementation of augmentation on the same network that produced an accuracy of 0.9834 with a loss of 1.1e-05 was an accuracy of 0.9911 (final checkpoint) and a loss of 0.004. Not only was the 99% accuracy barrier broken but the memorization of the dataset was essentially blocked.

There seems to be diminishing returns along the axes tried. The augmentation for example increased the variation in the dataset close to 10 times but only saw a 0.0019 increase in the test accuracy. The remaining error may not be best improved by a simple scaling of the current axes; they may be the ones a pixel-independent architecture finds difficult to resolve. An MLP treats each pixel as independent from each other. It has no notion that neighboring pixels form lines and edges. This is why a convolutional neural network may be worth implementing and it is the natural next chapter for this project.

## 6. Conclusions and Next Steps

This project accomplished multiple things. It created a from-scratch NumPy neural network library with verified (to ~7 decimals) backpropagation. It produced empirically backed findings including Momentum's superiority over Adam and SGD for MNIST, relationship between batch and lr and its limits, throughput measurement and utilization optimization, and a diagnosis driven campaign that led to data augmentation which was used to reach an accuracy of 99.11%.

There were also many transferable lessons learned through this project. I learned the importance of reading numbers in diagnosing a problem. I learned to replicate the data before jumping to conclusions. I learned to verify what the code actually does especially in debugging. I learned that the scaling rules such as the relationship between batch size and lr is limited, and lower precision only pays with supporting computer architecture. I also learned that it is important to carve a validation set from training data so the test set is only used at the very end when testing the network.

For future plans, using CNNs to further increase the accuracy on the MNIST data set seems like a natural next step. After CNNs, implementing transformer architecture is key for deepening my knowledge in modern Deep Learning.
